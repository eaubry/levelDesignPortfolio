projects

Level design and landscape architecture both ultimately come down to the same thing: User experience. Whether it's designing a place in real life or a new level in a video game, it's my job to create memorable adventures that not only looks nice, but take it a step further by weaving exceptional narrative, environmental interaction, and the magical feeling of being lost in another world.

Player Experience Storytelling World Building Commercial Experience

player experience

Why are video games an ever increasing popular alternative to books and TV? Well I believe that one giant factor is experience. In books, the reader can become immersed in the world that they read about and grow with the characters, but the plot is fixed and the outcome is predetermined. However, in video games, the player is in control of their own destiny (to an extent), and it is completely up to them whether the hero triumphantly defeats the baddies, or slips and falls down an endless pit. In this way, level design should be about guiding the player, not just holding their hand. Great level design still keeps the player on track if they get too lost, but that is delicately intertwined with allowing them to run free, explore, and do things they can't do in real life.

pokemon obsidian (rpgmaker)

One project I've been working on for the past 2 years is a custom Pokemon game, made for a mod for RPG Maker. Although most of the base code and assets were created by the Pokemon Essentials community, all story, level design, and in-game scripting were created entirely by me (I did do some custom coding and asset creation myself, too.)

I feel that this project is a great example of player experience. It keeps the charm of the original Pokemon games, but allows for much more exploration. Sidequests have been added (and tracked), towns have a larger amount of houses and residents (all of whom share their own personal stories), and the region has a rich backstory that is revealed as the player explores. (Oh, and did I mention you can actually catch 'em all? Because you can.)

storytelling

Another thing about level design is telling a story. You might be thinking, "hey isn't that Dave's job from the story design department across the hall?" Well, I mean, yes, but what are you making the level for then? The most successful levels are the ones in which the story flows through the level. I'm talking about dynamic levels that have completely changed after something important has happened. I'm talking about landscapes with subtle traces of the world's history or a person's backstory. I'm talking about dungeons that are there for a reason, and not just there to pad a game's play time.


the rennaissance man (unity)

What if I told you that Leonardo daVinci was actually a loser that failed at inventing things? But he becomes famous anyway by taking a stolen time machine through the ages to kill other inventors and take credit for their achievements? Well that's exactly what this game is about.


Growing up, I've always been a fan of 2D metroidvania style games and the original idea I came up with for Rennaissance Man went hand-in-hand with that gameplay. Each invention that the player steals can be used as an upgrade to reach other areas further in the game. This takes the usual item-progression formula, but mixes it with story elements that reward the player with a new chapter of the story, rather than just giving them a new item because they need it to continue.


I've been refining the story, characters, and level for over a year now (with multiple iterations), but have only just recently got into starting to program this game, so there isn't a lot of visual content to show. However, feel free to contact me and I will gladly send you the excel document which is basically the roadmap of the game, complete with ideas for 8 worlds and 40+ inventors! 

world building

When it comes to experience and storytelling, video game design and landscape architecture are no different. What sets them apart is the world building element. Speaking from personal experience as a landscape architect, it always feels good to design a space that is just awesome- perhaps a client wants a resort built with a winding path through a grove of trees that makes its way up to an alcove with a private oasis and waterfall. But... is it in the budget? Is it handicapped accessible? Are the trees the right species to grow in that climate? How will the water be drained during a thunderstorm? Constraints, constraints, constraints. When it comes to video games, anything goes (as long as it fits into the memory). And that's what I love about level design. You build the world to inspire, enchant, and fascinate the player without limitation. People play video games to do things they can't do in real life, so let the level do just that. (While we're at it, lets add a temple to that alcove. WIth living skeletons.)

commercial experience

Although I've been practicing design for almost a decade now, I've only been part of the video game industry for about 2 years. As such, the majority of my projects are small, unreleased files or they just aren't finished yet. However, as of just recently, I am proud to say that I finally have a publicly released mobile game that I developed entirely by myself (made in Unity). Although the game is small and relatively simple, I feel that I learned so much more about what goes into commercial releases than I ever would have learned through just unreleased projects.

mini monarchy (unity)

Mini monarchy is a mobile game developed by my own recent startup company, Mice n' DIce Games. The premise is simple: conquer lands and expand your empire as large as you can before dying out. The game itself utilizes a combination of strategy and luck, leading to short rounds of addictive gameplay.

Despite being such a simple game, a lot of work went into it, especially since I worked entirely on it myself. Coding, gameplay, art, and UI were all necessary components I had to develop, but the biggest hurdle was setting up all the legal things that come from releasing a public game. However, I feel the experience was worth it.
